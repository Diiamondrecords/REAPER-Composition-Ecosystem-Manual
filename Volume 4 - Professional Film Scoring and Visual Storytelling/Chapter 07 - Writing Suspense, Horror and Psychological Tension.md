Chapter 7 — Writing Suspense, Horror and Psychological Tension

"Fear is rarely created by what the audience sees. It is created by what they believe might happen next."

Introduction

Suspense and horror are among the most psychologically demanding forms of film scoring.

Unlike action music,

their purpose is not excitement.

Their purpose is uncertainty.

Professional suspense music manipulates expectation.

The audience constantly asks:

"What happens next?"

The score should encourage that question without answering it too soon.

Suspense Is Anticipation

Suspense exists before the event.

The audience senses danger long before it arrives.

Professional composers build anticipation using:

Slow harmonic movement.
Repeating figures.
Sparse orchestration.
Unresolved harmony.
Silence.
Horror Is Psychological

Many beginning composers think horror means:

Loud dissonance.

Sudden orchestral hits.

Extreme dynamics.

Professional horror often uses restraint.

Examples

One sustained cello.

↓

Low piano.

↓

Soft breathing textures.

↓

Whispers.

↓

Silence.

The imagination is usually more frightening than obvious musical violence.

Harmonic Ambiguity

Avoid immediate resolution.

Useful techniques include:

Minor seconds.
Tritones.
Cluster chords.
Quartal harmony.
Whole-tone movement.
Chromatic bass lines.

These create instability without requiring constant loudness.

Minimalism

Professional suspense cues often contain very little material.

One note.

↓

One rhythm.

↓

One texture.

↓

One pulse.

Repeating simple ideas allows tension to build gradually.

Drones

A drone is a sustained pitch beneath changing textures.

Drones create:

Instability.

Expectation.

Psychological discomfort.

Very small harmonic changes become highly noticeable.

Rhythmic Uncertainty

Avoid predictable rhythmic patterns.

Instead use:

Uneven phrases.

Unexpected rests.

Delayed accents.

Irregular ostinatos.

The audience loses rhythmic certainty.

Silence

Silence is one of the strongest suspense tools.

Examples

Music stops.

↓

Audience waits.

↓

Nothing happens.

↓

Then...

The score returns.

Silence increases attention.

Delayed Resolution

Never resolve tension immediately.

Allow suspense to breathe.

Examples

Unresolved harmony.

↓

False resolution.

↓

Greater tension.

↓

Final release.

The audience remains emotionally engaged.

Character Psychology

Rather than scoring the monster,

score the character's emotional experience.

Fear.

Confusion.

Isolation.

Paranoia.

Music becomes more human and therefore more effective.

Composer's Exercise

Write a three-minute suspense cue.

Use:

Only five notes.
One recurring rhythm.
Two orchestral families.
No traditional cadence until the ending.

Observe how limitation increases dramatic tension.

Professional Suspense Checklist

Before completing a suspense cue ask:

✓ Does uncertainty remain?

✓ Is harmony intentionally unresolved?

✓ Does silence contribute?

✓ Are orchestral colors restrained?

✓ Is tension gradually increasing?

✓ Does the audience anticipate rather than simply react?

✓ Is the final release earned?

Chapter Summary

Professional suspense scoring means:

Building anticipation rather than shock.
Using harmonic ambiguity.
Employing silence strategically.
Controlling psychological pacing.
Writing for emotional experience rather than visible action.
Delaying resolution carefully.

The greatest suspense scores rarely frighten through volume.

They frighten through expectation.

"The audience fears most what the music suggests—but never fully reveals."
