Chapter 2 — Spotting a Film and Building the Musical Narrative

"Before writing the first note, professional film composers first decide where music should—and should not—exist."

Introduction

One of the biggest differences between writing music and writing film music is a process called spotting.

Beginning composers often want to score every second of a film.

Professional composers know that constant music often reduces emotional impact.

Silence has value.

Dialogue has value.

Sound effects have value.

Music should enter only when it contributes something unique.

Spotting is the art of deciding where music belongs.

What Is A Spotting Session?

A spotting session is when the composer and director watch the film together.

Rather than discussing musical notes,

they discuss dramatic intention.

Typical questions include:

Where should music begin?
Where should it stop?
What emotion should this cue communicate?
Should the audience notice the music?
Should the cue remain subtle?

Professional spotting determines the emotional roadmap before composing begins.

Music Must Earn Its Place

Never assume every scene requires music.

Ask:

Would silence communicate this moment better?

Sometimes the absence of music creates greater emotional power than the most beautiful orchestration.

Professionals are comfortable leaving scenes completely unscored.

Identifying Emotional Beats

Every scene contains emotional events.

Examples

A realization.

↓

A surprise.

↓

A decision.

↓

A loss.

↓

A victory.

Music should react to emotional beats—not simply visual movement.

Enter Late, Leave Early

A common professional principle is:

Enter later than expected.

Leave earlier than expected.

Allow dialogue and natural sound to establish the scene before introducing music.

Likewise,

allow important emotional moments to resonate after the music ends.

The Emotional Arc of a Scene

Every cue should have its own miniature journey.

Beginning.

↓

Development.

↓

Climax.

↓

Resolution.

Even a thirty-second cue benefits from emotional structure.

Scoring the Subtext

Professional film composers often write for what characters are feeling,

not what they are saying.

Example

A character smiles.

The dialogue seems cheerful.

Yet internally,

the character is grieving.

The music may reflect the hidden emotion rather than the visible action.

Scene Transitions

Film cues rarely exist in isolation.

Ask:

How does one cue connect to the next?

Professional scores often use:

Shared motifs.

Common harmonies.

Sustained textures.

Gradual orchestration changes.

These create continuity across the film.

Musical Perspective

Whose perspective does the cue represent?

Examples

The hero.

↓

The audience.

↓

The villain.

↓

The environment.

Changing perspective changes the score dramatically.

Cue Length

Longer is not always better.

Sometimes:

15 seconds

communicates more effectively than

90 seconds.

Professional composers write only what the scene requires.

Composer's Exercise

Choose a five-minute scene from a film.

Without composing,

watch it three times.

First viewing:

Write where music should begin and end.

Second viewing:

Mark every emotional beat.

Third viewing:

Describe the emotional objective of every cue.

Only then begin writing music.

Professional Spotting Checklist

Before composing ask:

✓ Why does this cue exist?

✓ What emotion should it communicate?

✓ Does silence work better?

✓ Where should the cue begin?

✓ Where should it end?

✓ Whose perspective does the music represent?

✓ How does this cue connect to the next?

Chapter Summary

Professional spotting means:

Scoring emotion rather than action.
Allowing silence to remain meaningful.
Building cues around emotional beats.
Supporting dramatic storytelling.
Connecting cues into one continuous musical narrative.
Planning before composing.

Great film music begins long before the first note is written.

It begins by understanding the story.

"The greatest film composers do not ask where music can be added. They ask where music is truly needed."
