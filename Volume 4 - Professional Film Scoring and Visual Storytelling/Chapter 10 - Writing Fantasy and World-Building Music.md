Chapter 10 — Writing Fantasy and World-Building Music

"Fantasy music does not merely accompany an imaginary world. It convinces the audience that the world truly exists."

Introduction

Fantasy scoring is one of the richest forms of cinematic composition.

Unlike realistic drama,

fantasy often asks the composer to create the musical identity of an entirely new world.

The audience has never visited this kingdom.

Never met these creatures.

Never heard these cultures.

The music becomes part of the world's architecture.

Professional fantasy composers write music that feels ancient, believable, and emotionally authentic.

Music Builds Worlds

Every fictional world has its own musical language.

Ask yourself:

What history does this world have?
What cultures live here?
What instruments might exist?
Is the world peaceful or dangerous?
Is magic common or mysterious?

These questions influence every musical decision.

Ancient Civilizations

Older civilizations often benefit from:

Open fifths.

Modal harmony.

Deep percussion.

Male choir.

Long melodic phrases.

The audience subconsciously senses age and history.

Nature

Nature themes often feature:

Woodwinds.

Flowing strings.

Gentle harp.

Bird-like motifs.

Open harmony.

The music breathes naturally.

Villainous Kingdoms

Dark worlds often use:

Low brass.

Low strings.

Minor seconds.

Chromatic movement.

Heavy percussion.

Sparse choir.

Avoid making every cue loud.

Quiet darkness is often more effective.

Invent one fictional kingdom.

Professional Fantasy Checklist

Before completing a cue ask:

✓ Does the music define the world?

✓ Does every culture have an identity?

✓ Is orchestration believable?

✓ Does harmony support the setting?

✓ Are recurring themes recognizable?

✓ Does magic sound unique?

✓ Does the score create one unified musical universe?

Chapter Summary

Professional fantasy scoring means:

Building believable musical worlds.
Creating distinct cultural identities.
Using orchestration as storytelling.
Developing recurring themes.
Choosing harmonic language carefully.
Making imaginary worlds feel emotionally real.

Fantasy music succeeds when the audience believes the world has existed long before the story began.

"The greatest fantasy scores do not transport listeners to another world. They make listeners believe they have finally returned to one they somehow always knew existed."
