Chapter 6 — Writing Action Music and Managing Energy

"Great action music is not merely fast. It is controlled energy directed toward a dramatic purpose."

Introduction

Action scoring is often misunderstood.

Beginning composers believe action music simply requires:

Fast tempo.
Loud orchestra.
Heavy percussion.

Professional composers understand that action music is really about energy management.

The audience should experience:

Urgency.

Momentum.

Danger.

Excitement.

Relief.

Victory.

Every action cue tells an emotional story rather than simply accompanying movement.

Action Begins Before Movement

The audience should often feel action before it happens.

Professional composers create anticipation using:

Ostinatos.
Harmonic tension.
Low percussion.
Rising strings.
Sustained brass.

Energy begins before the first explosion.

Ostinatos

An ostinato is a repeating musical pattern.

Examples:

Repeating string figures.
Piano patterns.
Synth pulses.
Repeated brass rhythms.

A good ostinato creates forward momentum without becoming distracting.

Percussion

Percussion provides impact.

Each instrument has a different role.

Examples

Taikos

↓

Massive power.

Timpani

↓

Weight.

Snare

↓

Military precision.

Cymbals

↓

Transitions.

Tom Drums

↓

Movement.

Professional percussion supports—not overwhelms—the orchestra.

Harmonic Simplicity

Action scenes rarely require highly complex harmony.

Instead,

professional composers often use:

Pedal tones.
Modal harmony.
Suspended chords.
Minor tonal centers.
Repeating harmonic cells.

Complexity comes from orchestration and rhythm rather than frequent chord changes.

Professional action music uses multiple rhythmic layers.

Example

↓

Pulse.

Bass

↓

Groove.

Strings

↓

Ostinato.

Brass

↓

Accents.

Choir

↓

Long phrases.

Each layer contributes independently.

Controlling Intensity

Action music should evolve.

Beginning

↓

Preparation.

Middle

↓

Escalation.

Peak

↓

Maximum intensity.

Ending

↓

Release.

Constant maximum intensity quickly becomes exhausting.

Musical Direction

Ask:

Where is the cue going?

Every section should move toward a destination.

Professional action cues constantly build momentum.

Silence During Action

Sometimes removing the orchestra creates greater impact.

Examples

Explosion

↓

Silence.

↓

Single sustained note.

↓

Full orchestra returns.

Contrast increases dramatic power.

Synchronization

Action cues frequently synchronize with visual events.

Examples

Jump.

↓

Brass hit.

Door opens.

↓

Percussion accent.

Final strike.

↓

Orchestral climax.

Synchronization should feel natural rather than mechanical.

Maintaining Themes

Even during action,

themes remain important.

Instead of abandoning the character's motif,

transform it.

Examples

Hero Theme

↓

Fast rhythm.

↓

Minor mode.

↓

Heavy brass.

↓

Aggressive percussion.

The audience still recognizes the character.

Composer's Exercise

Write a two-minute action cue.

Divide it into four sections.

Section 1

Preparation.

Section 2

Escalation.

Section 3

Maximum Action.

Section 4

Resolution.

Keep one character theme present throughout the entire cue.

Professional Action Cue Checklist

Before completing an action cue ask:

✓ Does energy build naturally?

✓ Is there enough contrast?

✓ Are percussion and orchestra balanced?

✓ Does harmony support momentum?

✓ Does the character theme remain recognizable?

✓ Are visual events supported?

✓ Does the cue end with satisfying resolution?

Chapter Summary

Professional action scoring means:

Building anticipation before movement.
Using ostinatos effectively.
Managing rhythmic layers.
Controlling intensity.
Supporting visual storytelling.
Preserving thematic identity during action.

Great action music is not simply loud or fast.

It is carefully controlled emotional momentum.

"The most exciting action cues are not those with the most notes, but those that guide the audience through perfectly controlled waves of tension and release."
