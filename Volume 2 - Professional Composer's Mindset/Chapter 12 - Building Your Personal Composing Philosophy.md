Chapter 12 — Building Your Personal Composing Philosophy

"Every great composer eventually stops asking, 'How do others write music?' and begins asking, 'What do I believe music should be?'"

Introduction

Every composer eventually reaches a crossroads.

At first, we imitate.

We study theory.

We analyze scores.

We learn workflows.

We practice techniques.

Eventually, however, another question appears.

What kind of composer do I want to become?

This chapter explores how professionals gradually build a personal composing philosophy.

Beyond Technique

Technique teaches you how to write.

Philosophy teaches you why you write.

Both are essential.

Without technique...

Ideas remain trapped.

Without philosophy...

Music lacks identity.

Every Composer Has Beliefs

Whether consciously or unconsciously, every composer develops beliefs.

Examples

"I believe melody should always come first."

"I believe rhythm is the foundation."

"I believe harmony creates emotion."

"I believe simplicity communicates best."

None of these are universally correct.

They are artistic choices.

Discovering Your Priorities

Ask yourself:

What matters most to me?

Examples

Emotion

Melody

Groove

Harmony

Texture

Story

Energy

Atmosphere

The answer becomes your artistic compass.

The Difference Between Style and Philosophy

Style is how your music sounds.

Philosophy is why you make certain decisions.

Example

Style

Neo Soul.

Philosophy

"I value emotional harmony above technical complexity."

Style may change.

Philosophy usually evolves much more slowly.

Learning Without Copying

Study your favorite composers.

Do not copy their music.

Study their thinking.

Ask:

Why did they choose this harmony?

Why did they delay the climax?

Why is this melody so memorable?

Understanding decisions is more valuable than copying results.

Writing Your Own Rules

Professionals often develop personal guidelines.

Examples

Finish every composition.

Never introduce an instrument without purpose.

Always develop themes before replacing them.

Keep choruses emotionally stronger than verses.

These are not universal laws.

They are personal principles.

Evolving Naturally

Your philosophy should evolve.

A belief you hold today may change after writing fifty more compositions.

Growth is expected.

Remain curious.

The Role of Experimentation

Sometimes your philosophy grows through failure.

A composition that doesn't work often teaches more than one that does.

Do not fear experiments.

Document what you learn.

Building Your Mission Statement

Complete these sentences.

I compose because...

I hope listeners feel...

The musical element I value most is...

My compositions should always...

The emotion I enjoy writing most is...

Return to these answers once a year.

Watch them evolve.

The Composer's Journal

Create one page after every completed composition.

Answer:

What did I believe before writing this piece?

What did I believe after finishing it?

What surprised me?

What principle became stronger?

What principle changed?

Over time this journal becomes your personal philosophy book.

Avoid Dogma

Never believe there is only one correct way to compose.

Music is too large.

Remain open.

Continue learning.

Let your philosophy guide you—

not limit you.

Composer's Exercise

Write ten personal composing principles.

Examples

Melody must always communicate.
Silence is as important as sound.
Simplicity is powerful.
Every section must have purpose.
Emotion comes before complexity.

Read these principles before beginning every major project.

Chapter Summary

Professional composers:

Develop personal artistic beliefs.
Study decisions instead of imitation.
Build philosophy through experience.
Allow their ideas to evolve.
Define success according to their own artistic goals.
Let every composition strengthen their understanding of music.

Eventually, your philosophy becomes the invisible thread connecting every piece you write.

"Technique teaches your hands what to do. Philosophy teaches your imagination why it matters."
