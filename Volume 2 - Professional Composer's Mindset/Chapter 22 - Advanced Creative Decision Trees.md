Chapter 22 — Advanced Creative Decision Trees

"The fastest professional decisions are rarely guesses. They follow an internal decision tree built from years of experience."

Introduction

One of the greatest differences between beginning and professional composers is speed of decision making.

A beginner might spend thirty minutes wondering:

Should this be major?

Minor?

Should I add strings?

Should the bass move?

Should I repeat the melody?

A professional often answers these questions in seconds.

Why?

Because they have developed mental decision trees.

This chapter teaches you how to build those decision trees for your own composing process.

What Is A Decision Tree?

A decision tree is a logical sequence of questions.

Instead of randomly experimenting,

you ask the next appropriate question.

Example

Does the melody communicate the emotion?

↓

YES

↓

Develop it.

↓

NO

↓

Rewrite it.

Simple.

Fast.

Repeatable.

Building Your Own Decision Trees

As you gain experience,

write your own.

Example

Whenever my chorus feels weak...

↓

Check melody.

↓

Check contrast.

↓

Check arrangement.

↓

Check bass.

↓

Check harmony.

↓

Only then rewrite.

Eventually this becomes automatic.

The Professional Shortcut

Professionals rarely ask:

"What should I try?"

Instead they ask:

"What category of problem is this?"

That one question immediately eliminates dozens of unnecessary possibilities.

Composer's Exercise

Create one decision tree for each of these:

Melody

Harmony

Bass

Arrangement

Song Form

Write them on one page.

Keep them beside your computer while composing.

After several months,

you'll rarely need to look at them.

They'll become part of your intuition.

Chapter Summary

Professional composers:

Organize musical decisions logically.
Solve problems systematically.
Build repeatable workflows.
Avoid random experimentation.
Develop personal decision trees through experience.
Gradually transform analysis into intuition.

Decision trees do not reduce creativity.

They free creativity by removing unnecessary uncertainty.

"The professional mind is not faster because it thinks harder. It is faster because it knows which question to ask next."
