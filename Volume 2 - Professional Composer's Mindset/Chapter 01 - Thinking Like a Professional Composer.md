Chapter 1 — Thinking Like a Professional Composer

"Volume 1 taught you how to compose. Volume 2 teaches you how professionals think while composing."

Introduction

There is a moment in every composer's life when writing notes is no longer the hardest part.

The challenge becomes making better musical decisions.

Two composers may know the same scales.

The same harmony.

The same orchestration.

The same software.

Yet one consistently writes compelling music while the other struggles.

Why?

Because professional composing is less about knowledge than it is about thinking.

This volume explores that way of thinking.

The Three Levels of Composing

Every composition exists on three different levels.

Level One
Notes

These are the individual pitches, rhythms and chords.

This is where beginners spend most of their attention.

Level Two
Relationships

Professionals think less about individual notes and more about relationships.

Questions include:

How does this melody relate to the harmony?
How does the bass support the groove?
How does the arrangement shape the emotional arc?
How does this section prepare the next one?

Music begins to feel connected rather than assembled.

Level Three
Purpose

At the highest level, every decision answers one question:

Why is this here?

Every note.

Every chord.

Every silence.

Every instrument.

Every transition.

Every variation.

Nothing is accidental.

The Professional Mindset

A professional composer rarely asks:

"What should I write next?"

Instead they ask:

What is the music missing?
What problem am I solving?
What emotion needs to become clearer?
What should the listener feel at this exact moment?

These questions lead to stronger decisions.

Each layer strengthens the one before it.

Thinking in Time

Beginners often think about what is happening now.

Professionals think about:

What happened before?
What is happening now?
What should happen next?

Every musical event prepares another.

Thinking in Contrast

Music without contrast quickly becomes predictable.

Professionals constantly balance:

Loud / Soft
Dense / Sparse
High / Low
Tension / Release
Simple / Complex
Familiar / Unexpected

Contrast keeps listeners engaged.

Thinking in Expectations

Every listener forms expectations.

A composer decides whether to:

Confirm them.
Delay them.
Break them.
Reward them.

This is the foundation of musical storytelling.

Thinking in Energy

Instead of asking,

"Is this section good?"

Ask,

"How much energy should this section have?"

Example:

Intro         15%

Verse         30%

Pre-Chorus    50%

Chorus        80%

Bridge        45%

Final Chorus 100%

Outro         10%

Energy planning often solves arrangement problems before they appear.

Thinking in Functions

Every musical element has a function.

Examples:

Melody

→ Identity

Bass

→ Movement

Harmony

→ Emotion

Rhythm

→ Motion

Arrangement

→ Story

Instrumentation

→ Color

When two elements perform the same function unnecessarily, one can often be simplified or removed.

Instead of trying to solve every problem yourself at once, let each tool help you focus on one category of decisions.

Composer's Exercise

Take one of your previous compositions.

Listen without editing.

Write down:

The strongest decision you made.
The weakest decision you made.
One decision you would make differently today.

Do not change the composition yet.

Simply learn from it.

Chapter Summary

Professional composers:

Think in purpose before notes.
Build music layer by layer.
Use contrast intentionally.
Plan energy over time.
Solve one musical problem at a time.
Judge every decision by its contribution to the listener's experience.

This mindset forms the foundation for everything in Volume 2.

"Great compositions are not the result of perfect inspiration. They are the result of consistently making thoughtful musical decisions."
