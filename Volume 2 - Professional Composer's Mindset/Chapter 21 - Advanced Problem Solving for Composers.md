Chapter 21 — Advanced Problem Solving for Composers

"Professional composers don't avoid problems. They solve them systematically."

Introduction

Every composer encounters problems.

Sometimes the melody refuses to develop.

Sometimes the harmony feels repetitive.

Sometimes the chorus lacks impact.

Sometimes nothing seems wrong...

...yet nothing feels right.

The difference between an amateur and a professional is not the absence of problems.

It is the ability to diagnose and solve them efficiently.

This chapter introduces a systematic approach to musical problem solving.

Symptoms vs Causes

One of the biggest mistakes composers make is treating symptoms instead of causes.

Example

Problem

"The chorus feels weak."

Possible Causes

Weak melody.
Weak harmonic contrast.
Weak arrangement.
Weak bass movement.
Weak emotional preparation.
Lack of dynamic growth.

The symptom is obvious.

The cause requires investigation.

The Professional Diagnosis Method

Before changing anything, ask:

What is actually wrong?

Then ask:

Why is it happening?

Only then begin editing.

The Five Why Method

Example

The chorus feels small.

↓

Why?

Not enough contrast.

↓

Why?

The verse is already too dense.

↓

Why?

Too many instruments entered too early.

↓

Why?

The arrangement wasn't planned.

↓

Solution

Simplify the verse instead of making the chorus bigger.

Never Guess

Professionals rarely edit randomly.

Instead they isolate one problem.

Test one solution.

Listen.

Evaluate.

Repeat.

Fix The Foundation First

Imagine painting a house with a cracked foundation.

The paint will never solve the real problem.

Music works the same way.

Always solve higher-level problems before lower-level ones.

The Elimination Method

When uncertain,

remove elements.

Mute:

Bass.

Pads.

Percussion.

Countermelody.

Strings.

Notice what changes.

Sometimes discovering what the music doesn't need is easier than deciding what to add.

One Change Rule

Change one thing.

Listen.

Evaluate.

Never change ten things simultaneously.

Otherwise you won't know which decision improved the music.

The Listener Test

Ask:

Would the average listener notice this problem?

If the answer is no,

perhaps it is not the highest priority.

Professionals solve the problems that affect communication first.

The Composer's Toolbox

As your experience grows,

you'll recognize problems much faster.

Eventually you'll think:

"This is a harmony problem."

"This is an arrangement problem."

"This is a bass problem."

"This is a structural problem."

Chapter Summary

Professional composers:

Diagnose before editing.
Solve causes instead of symptoms.
Improve one problem at a time.
Use structured workflows.
Test solutions objectively.
Trust listening more than assumptions.

Every problem is simply another opportunity to improve your craft.

"The best composers are not those who never encounter problems. They are the ones who know how to solve them."
