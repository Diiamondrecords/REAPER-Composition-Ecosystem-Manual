Chapter 21 — Writing for Piano, Harp and Keyboard Instruments

"The orchestra sings through many voices. The piano thinks. The harp dreams."

Introduction

Keyboard instruments occupy a unique place in orchestration.

Unlike most orchestral instruments, they can simultaneously perform:

Melody.
Harmony.
Bass.
Rhythm.
Counterpoint.

Because of this, they are often called complete instruments.

Professional orchestrators use keyboards not because they are convenient, but because each possesses an unmistakable emotional personality.

The Piano

The piano is perhaps the most versatile instrument ever created.

Characteristics:

Honest.
Intimate.
Powerful.
Transparent.

Excellent for:

Drama.

Romance.

Reflection.

Memory.

Psychological storytelling.

Unlike many orchestral instruments,

the piano can function alone or as part of the orchestra.

Solo Piano

A solo piano immediately creates intimacy.

Excellent for:

Character themes.

Flashbacks.

Opening scenes.

Closing credits.

Quiet emotional moments.

Professional composers often begin an entire score from a piano sketch.

Common Mistakes

Avoid:

Constant piano throughout every cue.

Overusing harp glissandi.

Writing unrealistic harp passages.

Using celesta for every magical moment.

Allowing keyboards to overpower orchestral balance.

Professional restraint produces elegance.

Composer's Exercise

Compose one sixteen-bar orchestral theme.

Arrange it four ways:

Solo Piano.
Piano and Strings.
Harp and Flute.
Celesta with High Strings.

Keep every note identical.

Observe how the emotional atmosphere changes purely through instrumental color.

Professional Keyboard Checklist

Before completing a cue ask:

✓ Is the keyboard instrument emotionally appropriate?

✓ Does it support rather than dominate?

✓ Is the register effective?

✓ Are keyboard textures enhancing the orchestra?

✓ Are harp techniques realistic?

✓ Is the orchestration balanced?

✓ Would silence strengthen the keyboard entrance?

Chapter Summary

Professional keyboard orchestration means:

Understanding the emotional role of each keyboard instrument.
Using piano for intimacy and structure.
Employing harp for elegance and motion.
Reserving celesta for wonder and magic.
Using organ thoughtfully for grandeur.
Blending keyboards naturally into the orchestral texture.

The greatest keyboard writing does not exist apart from the orchestra.

It becomes another expressive voice within the larger musical conversation.

"The piano reveals the composer's thoughts. The harp reveals their dreams. Together they remind us that orchestration is as much about color as it is about notes."
